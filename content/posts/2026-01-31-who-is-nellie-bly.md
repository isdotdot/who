Nellie Bly was a pioneering American journalist known for her daring reporting style and determination to expose social injustices during the late 19th century. Born Elizabeth Jane Cochrane on May 5, 1864, in Armstrong County, Pennsylvania, she went on to become one of the most celebrated female journalists of her time.

### Early Life

Born into a modest Irish-American family, Nellie Bly's early life was marked by tragedy when her father died suddenly. Her mother, Mary Jane Cochrane, struggled to make ends meet while raising Elizabeth and her siblings. To support her family, Elizabeth began working as a domestic servant at the age of 11. However, she soon discovered that she had a talent for writing and was encouraged by her uncle, David Parkes, who recognized her potential.

### Career

In 1885, at the age of 21, Nellie Bly moved to New York City with little more than $50 in her pocket. She began working as a writer for the Pittsburgh Dispatch, where she quickly gained recognition for her clever and engaging writing style. In 1887, she joined the New York World, one of the most prominent newspapers of its time, where she would remain for several years.

### Context and Impact

During the late 19th century, women's roles in society were limited, and the field of journalism was particularly male-dominated. Nellie Bly's determination to succeed and make a name for herself in this industry paved the way for future generations of female journalists. Her fearless approach to reporting and willingness to take risks inspired others to do the same.

Nellie Bly's influence extended beyond her own career, as she helped raise awareness about important social issues such as women's rights, labor laws, and child welfare. Her writing continues to be studied by scholars and journalism students today for its impact on investigative reporting and the role of journalists in shaping public opinion.
